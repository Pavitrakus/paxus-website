## THE STORY

Honestly, it started out of frustration.

We were students at a time when everyone around us was preparing for placements, internships, the usual path. Nothing wrong with that. But we kept running into the same feeling — that the tools being built for India, the software, the AI, the platforms — they were being built by people who had never really lived here. Not in Kanpur. Not in the tier-2 cities where most of the country actually lives.

The products felt foreign. The assumptions were wrong. The AI didn't understand the problems.

So we started building.

Not with a grand plan. Not with a thesis written on a whiteboard. We started building because we couldn't find what we were looking for, and at some point you stop looking and start making.

The first thing we built didn't work.
The second thing almost did.
By the third, we had paying customers and a lot of very uncomfortable conversations about what exactly we were trying to do.

That's when PaXus became real — not as a company, but as a commitment. Three of us — me, Aditya, Tanish — decided we weren't interested in building one product and hoping it worked. We were interested in something bigger: building the infrastructure layer that India's next decade runs on, across whatever verticals needed it most.

That sounds ambitious. It is.

But the model has a name. Bill Gross started doing it in 1996 — testing multiple ideas under one roof, killing the ones that don't work, and doubling down on the ones that do. He called it a venture studio. We just called it the only honest way we knew how to build.

What we've built so far is not polished.
Some of it is messy. All of it is real.

**Vivacity** — we watched edtech in India spend billions on content that looked like it was made in 2009. Talking heads, static slides, zero engagement. We built AI that generates animated, curriculum-grade video from a text prompt. The kind of content that actually teaches. Targeting the platforms serving hundreds of millions of Indian students.

**ORCA** — we kept meeting people, usually small business owners, who needed a phone agent but couldn't afford one, couldn't configure one, didn't trust one. We built an AI that controls your Android through WhatsApp. No apps. No setup. Just message it and it does the work. People are already paying for it.

**ByteForge** — we noticed the students building the next generation of Indian tech had nowhere to actually learn from each other. Not in a classroom. In the messy, collaborative way that real building happens. 4,500 members later, we have an MOU with IIT Kanpur Gymkhana and more demand than we know what to do with.

And in parallel — we are doing serious research on secure computation with Professor Adithya Vadapalli at IIT Kanpur. Because the AI systems of the future need to be built on cryptographic foundations that actually hold. We want to be part of building that too.

---

Are these connected?

Yes. In the way that matters.

They're all answers to the same question: what does intelligent software look like when it's built for India, by people from India, who aren't leaving?

We are not a startup that pivoted three times and landed somewhere safe. We are a venture studio — building, testing, killing, doubling down — with a singular obsession at the center of everything.

The obsession is this:

**India's next infrastructure will be intelligent.
And it will be built from within.**

We're backed by gradCapital.
We were incubated at SIIC, IIT Kanpur.
We are based in Kanpur, Uttar Pradesh.

We are three founders who are not waiting for the right moment.

We decided a long time ago that the right moment is now.
